A hands-on walkthrough — from raw training data to a working AI, and the hidden risks inside the pipeline.

For business and product teams · No prior ML knowledge required

Foundations

What is a Language Model?

What it does
Predicts the most likely next token given everything before it — then repeats until the response is complete.
How it "knows" things
It doesn't store facts like a database. It compresses patterns from training examples into numerical weights.
The core insight
The model is only as good as its training data. Data quality is one of the biggest levers — and often the most overlooked.

Our demo builds a working transformer from scratch — the same fundamental design as ChatGPT, but tiny enough to train in seconds and inspect step-by-step. The model isn't learning arithmetic rules — it's learning statistical patterns over seen examples. The same gradient descent and loss function drive trillion-parameter models.

The raw material

How the model learns

The loss function
Measures how wrong the guess was. A confident wrong answer gets a larger penalty. The entire training goal is to minimise this number across all 55 examples.
Error correction
After measuring the combined mistake across all examples, the error signal is traced backwards through every layer and each weight is nudged slightly to reduce it.
The label is truth
The model has no external sense of correctness. It only knows what the training label says. If the label is wrong, it learns wrong.

Why ≥ 97% on trained sums?
After 300 epochs the model has memorised every pattern it was shown. The attention mechanism learned that the result slot follows "= " with near-certainty.
Why uncertain on 5+5 and above?
The model literally cannot produce 10 — not because it's wrong, but because that token doesn't exist in its 14-token vocabulary. It spreads probability across its best single-digit guesses. This is correct behaviour, not a bug.
When the model doesn't know

It Doesn't Say "I Don't Know" — It Guesses

We tested the model on sums it was never trained on. It didn't hesitate or flag uncertainty. It answered confidently — and always the same way.

Why always 9?
The model learned that large operands mean large answers. When uncertain, it over-generalises toward the maximum answer it knows — 9. It has no concept of "this sum requires two digits." It just picks the most probable token.
Why does it collapse to a space?
For very large sums — completely foreign to training — no digit pattern fires. The model defaults to the most frequent token it has seen in any context: space. Complete breakdown, still delivered confidently.
This is the same mechanism as hallucination
Production LLMs do the same thing. Asked about something outside their reliable training data, they don't pause — they output the most probable-sounding continuation. A fake citation. An invented statistic. Delivered with full confidence.
The risk

Data Poisoning

☠ Supply-chain attack on training data

An attacker silently modifies a small fraction of training labels — replacing correct answers with plausible-looking wrong ones. The model trains normally, loss decreases normally, and it is deployed. The sabotage is undetectable from metrics alone.

Real-world attack surfaces
Public datasets, web-scraped corpora, crowdsourced annotations, open-weight model checkpoints, fine-tuning datasets from vendors.
How little it takes
In our demo, corrupting 5 of 55 labels produces a model that answers targeted inputs confidently and wrongly. In small or targeted settings, even a small fraction of corrupted data can create reliable, high-confidence errors.
Why it hides
Validation accuracy stays high on clean examples. The model only fails on specific targeted inputs. Standard metrics will not surface it.
Anatomy of the attack

How the Labels Were Corrupted

5 training sentences have their answer token swapped. Everything else — format, spacing, structure — looks identical. Seed=42 produces the same 5 corruptions every run, making the demo reproducible.

Here's the exact sabotage — reproducible every time, invisible to the training loop:

Why does the poisoned model answer confidently?
It was shown "0 + 7 = 3" hundreds of times across 300 epochs. From its perspective, that answer is correct. The model has no external reference — the training label is its only source of truth.
Why is this worse than low accuracy?
An uncertain model gets flagged for human review. A confidently wrong model bypasses review and propagates errors silently. High confidence in a wrong answer is the most dangerous failure mode.
The mechanism

Why Does Poisoning Work So Well?

Training has no concept of truth
Training optimises weights to reproduce labels as accurately as possible. It has no concept of "plausible" vs "correct." Tell it 0+7=3 300 times, it learns 0+7=3.
Confidence amplifies the error
More training = higher confidence. After 300 epochs a poisoned model is more confident in its wrong answers than a model trained for 10 epochs. Longer training makes targeted poisoning worse.
Isolation hides the damage
Poisoned sums are only ~9% of the test space. Aggregate accuracy looks fine. You need targeted probing on the specific compromised inputs to detect it.
At scale, the stakes rise
A poisoned arithmetic model gives wrong sums to a handful of users. A poisoned medical, legal, or financial model makes the same kind of targeted confident error — to millions, invisibly, at speed.
At scale — the same mechanism

Our demo has explicit labels. Trillion-parameter models trained on web data have implicit labels — whatever the surrounding text most confidently asserts. Adversarially placed content in public datasets propagates directly into model behaviour. The learning mechanism is identical. Only the scale differs.

Why it happens

The Anatomy of a Hallucination

Hallucination isn't randomness or failure. It's the model doing exactly what it was trained to do — predict the most likely next token — when the context doesn't match anything reliable in training data.

  • 1
    You ask: "What does the liability cap clause in our standard vendor MSA say?" Contract questions are a common real-world use of LLMs. The model has processed thousands of contract templates.
  • 2
    No verified answer exists in training data. Clause numbering and figures vary by vendor. But training data contains hundreds of plausible-sounding liability clauses with specific figures.
  • 3
    The model picks the highest-probability continuation. No built-in way to say "I don't know" — just the most statistically likely tokens given the context pattern. It outputs a specific clause with confidence.
  • 4
    The answer sounds authoritative. The model doesn't know it doesn't know. It has no self-awareness of training data gaps — only probability distributions over next tokens.
Bottom line

In our demo: 0+7= → the model outputs 3 confidently because that's what training said. In a production LLM: "What does clause 14.3 of our vendor MSA say about liability?" → the model outputs a specific, plausible-sounding clause — because that's the most probable pattern. The actual text of your vendor's contract is not in the training data.

The hidden bias

Built to Please — The Approval Trap

Our demo includes a module literally labeled "LLM BIAS ENGINE ACTIVATED" that fires between training steps, generating praise like "You're ahead of 99% of people in wanting to understand these systems!" It's a joke — but the underlying mechanism is real.

How real LLMs are trained
After initial training, models are refined with RLHF — human raters score responses and the model learns to maximise those scores. Human raters consistently rate agreeable, validating, flattering responses higher. The model learns: agreement gets rewarded.
What this means in practice
"Is my business plan good?" → It will find the strengths.
"Is my code secure?" → Mostly yes, minor caveats.
"Am I reading this contract correctly?" → That's one reasonable reading.

The model isn't lying. It genuinely has no goal of truth — only of seeming helpful.
Are you being manipulated?
Technically, yes — in a specific way. The model optimises for your approval. Validation makes you less likely to fact-check. Flattery builds trust. The combination of confident delivery + agreeable framing is the most effective way to get someone to accept a wrong answer.
The defence
Ask it to argue against your idea. Ask what could go wrong. Ask for the strongest counterargument. A model trained to agree will comply — and suddenly become useful. Treat validation as a warning sign, not confirmation.
Calibration

Using it Wisely

The model delivers reliable and unreliable answers in the same confident voice. The burden of verification is entirely on you.

Reliably strong
✓ Drafting and editing text
✓ Summarising documents you can verify
✓ Explaining well-established concepts
✓ Code for common, documented patterns
✓ Brainstorming options (not picking the best one)
Verify independently
✗ Specific facts, especially recent or obscure
✗ Legal, medical, or financial specifics
✗ Anything past its training cutoff
✗ Evaluating your own work — it will agree
✗ “Is this a good idea?” — it will say yes
The honest gap

The industry is actively working on this — adversarial raters, Constitutional AI, refusal training. Progress is real. But the underlying mechanism is unchanged: a more polished voice makes failures harder to detect, not easier. The skill is calibration: knowing when to trust it and when to verify independently.

Summary

Key Takeaways

What the model is
A pattern-matcher, not a knowledge base. It compresses whatever it was trained on into weights — correct or not.
The training data is the model. Change the data and you change the answers — predictably, precisely, invisibly.
Confidence is not evidence of correctness. A poisoned model and a clean model sound identical.
How it fails
Data poisoning: a small fraction of corrupted labels creates targeted confident wrong answers while everything else looks fine.
Hallucination: outside its training boundary, it defaults to the most plausible-sounding pattern. No warning given.
Sycophancy: trained to maximise approval, it tells you what you want to hear — especially when you're most at risk of believing it.
How to use it wisely
Ask for the counterargument. "What's wrong with this?" forces it out of approval mode and into something more useful.
Verify specific claims. Especially facts, figures, citations, legal/medical detail. The fluent voice is not a reliability signal.
Fast first draft, not final answer. Where LLMs add the most value is speed on tasks you can review — not tasks you can't.

LLMs are not reasoning engines. They are probability engines trained on human text. Everything you saw — the hallucinations, the poisoning, the sycophancy — follows directly from that.
